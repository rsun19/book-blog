The Last Thing He Told Me by Laura Dave

Rating: 5/10

Frankly, this was one of the most overhyped books I have ever read, and I've read People We Meet On Vacation by Emily Henry. For how many ratings it has, it sucks. When I checked this book out, I couldn't help but notice the large "Reese's Book Club" sticker on the cover, which really cracked me up. I want to meet the people in this club, because it's already making me question the legitimacy of said club. Ok fine, I might be overreacting here — the book isn't terrible per say, just extraordinarily average. Like the University of Virginia dining hall food average. It doesn't give you food poisoning, but it isn't exactly a tasty meal either. I might be spoiled by the sudden twists in The One by John Marrs, but this book felt like a joke compared to it. The plot was bland and underwhelming — at times, Hannah seemed paranoid, since the author consistantly failed to show the true gravity of her situation. In many parts of the story, it felt as if the main characters were in a bubble, unaffected by the outside world, which downplayed the urgency of their problems. Like I get that you need this "bubble" for character development, but maybe you shouldn't waste my time and call this a thriller. Because it isn't, it's just a mystery book. Don't bother reading this book if you wan't some adrenaline-rushing moments, because there's barely any violence, police involvement, or any sense of urgency. It's just two people investigating a mystery, that's all.

Summary: When Owen Michaels is caught in a SEC scandal that could implicate him in fraud, he suddently disappears and instructs his wife, Hannah, to protect their child Bailey under any circumstances. He also leaves them a black duffel bag full of cash. Neither Hannah nor Bailey knows why, and they get visits from a US Marshal and two FBI agents. Hannah and Bailey then try feverishly to figure out why Owen disappeared, and they quickly realize that Owen isn't who they think he is. They need to figure out who Owen really is, and why he's in trouble. Because it sounds like he's in more trouble than any of them realize.

WARNING: CONTAINS SPOILERS!

Out of all of the thrillers that I've ever had, this was one of the most boring, ever. You're telling me that a huge cabal hellbent on getting revenge on Owen wouldn't try to kidnap Hannah or Bailey from the get-go? That they wouldn't at least make an attempt to ransom them for his surrender? My goodness, the lack of action in this book made the plot super dry and vanilla, and none of their "discoveries" seemed significant. Wouldn't it be fitting to have Grady killed, or to have an kidnapping attempt on Bailey to isolate them and advance the plot? I mean, Laura Dave doesn't seem like a bad writer, but she should have more exciting ideas like these. Also, when Hannah was running away from Charlie in the bar, I felt that there should have been more clarity on why she was running. It just made her seem so paranoid, especially since we didn't know about the super scary cabal. At least there was good character development with Laura and Bailey. Despite everything, at least I'm glad that Laura and Bailey are now on good terms with each other, and that Bailey knows that Laura loves her unconditionally like her father.

The last quarter of the book started to pick up, with Hannah finally meeting Nicholas. I liked the meeting between them and the tense atmosphere that Laura Dave cultivated, and I wished that she would have done intense scenes like this throughout the book. Furthermore, the ending was heartbreaking, though I liked Owen and Hannah's final goodbye, as it gave us the closure that we desperately needed. It's an unfair and bittersweet ending, but I liked the thought that was put into it. It's like the author feel asleep at the wheel for 3/4 of the book, then got the inspiration she needed to finish it off. Reading this book was truly a wacky experience.
